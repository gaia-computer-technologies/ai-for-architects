# /space-feel

Describe the experiential and phenomenological qualities of architectural spaces. Translate spatial design into felt experience using precise sensory language.

## When to use

- Writing room-by-room experience descriptions for a client or jury
- Communicating what a space will feel like before it exists
- Enriching render briefs or visualisation direction with spatial atmosphere
- Reviewing a design for its experiential coherence -- does the sequence of spaces create a legible emotional arc?

## Input

- Room or space description: dimensions, proportions, materials, apertures
- Position in the building sequence (entry, transition, destination, retreat)
- Intended use and mood
- Lighting conditions (orientation, time of day, artificial strategy)
- Material palette (surfaces the body encounters)

## Process

1. Establish the spatial archetype: is this space about compression or release, prospect or refuge, gathering or solitude?
2. Read the proportions: tall and narrow produces verticality and aspiration; long and low produces horizon and calm; cubic produces containment.
3. Trace the light: where does it enter, what surfaces does it hit first, how does it change through the day?
4. Identify the haptic encounters: what does the hand touch on entry (door handle), what does the foot meet (threshold), what does the body lean against?
5. Layer in acoustic quality: hard surfaces reflect and energise; soft surfaces absorb and quieten.
6. Write from the body moving through the space, not from an overhead plan.

## Domain knowledge

### Spatial concepts

**Compression and release**: the deliberate narrowing of a space (low ceiling, narrow corridor, restricted view) followed by expansion (double height, panoramic window, open plan). This is the most powerful tool in architectural experience. Tadao Ando's Church of the Light uses a dark, compressed entry to make the cruciform light slot overwhelming. Frank Lloyd Wright compressed entry halls to 2.1m before releasing into double-height living rooms.

**Prospect and refuge**: from environmental psychology (Appleton, 1975). Humans seek spaces that offer both a view outward (prospect -- a sense of overview and safety) and enclosure at the back (refuge -- protection). A window seat in a thick wall is pure prospect-refuge. A mezzanine overlooking a living room below. A covered terrace facing a landscape.

**Threshold**: the moment of transition between one spatial condition and another. Not just a door -- a change in floor level, ceiling height, material, light quality, or acoustic condition. Good thresholds are legible: you feel that you have crossed from one world into another. Scarpa's Castelvecchio is a masterclass in threshold.

**Enfilade**: a sequence of aligned doorways or openings that create a telescoping vista through multiple rooms. Produces depth, ceremony, and visual connection across distance. Baroque in origin, reinterpreted by SANAA (aligned but offset openings) and David Chipperfield (Neues Museum).

**Raumplan**: Adolf Loos's concept of interconnected rooms at different levels within a single volume, connected by short stairs. Each room has its own ceiling height calibrated to its function and importance. Produces spatial richness within a compact footprint. The Muller House is the canonical example.

**Poche**: the solid mass between rooms. In classical architecture, poche is thick and carved (niches, fireplaces, storage within walls). In modern architecture, poche often disappears, leaving rooms as thin-walled boxes. Recovering poche -- inhabiting the space between spaces -- creates depth, hidden storage, and thick thresholds.

**Ma (Japanese)**: the meaningful interval or pause between things. Not empty space but charged absence. A gap in a fence that frames a garden view. The silence between sounds. In architecture: the space between buildings matters as much as the buildings themselves.

### Sensory dimensions

**Light**
- Direct sunlight: sharp shadows, warmth on skin, moving patches that track the day. Creates drama and time-awareness.
- Diffuse light: even, shadowless, calming. Overcast sky through north-facing glazing. Good for work, contemplation.
- Reflected light: light bounced off a surface before entering -- off water (rippling), off a white wall (soft fill), off a coloured surface (tinted). Aalto bounced light off curved white ceilings. Barragan bounced it off coloured walls.
- Zenithal light: from above. The most powerful form -- churches, museums, bathhouses. Creates a sense of the sacred regardless of programme. Kahn's Kimbell, Zumthor's Therme Vals.
- Edge light: entering at the junction of wall and ceiling, or wall and floor. Dematerialises the surface it grazes. Creates a sense of weightlessness.

**Acoustics**
- Reverberant spaces (stone, concrete, glass, high ceilings): sound bounces, blurs, sustains. Creates grandeur but also fatigue. Churches, swimming pools, galleries.
- Absorptive spaces (timber, fabric, carpet, low ceilings): sound dies quickly. Creates intimacy, privacy, focus. Libraries, bedrooms, studios.
- The cocktail party problem: spaces designed for conversation need a reverberation time of 0.5-0.8 seconds. Below that feels dead; above that, you shout.
- Sound of materials: footsteps on stone (authoritative), on timber (warm, domestic), on concrete (industrial). These sonic signatures communicate programme before you consciously register them.

**Haptics**
- Temperature of surfaces: metal is cold to touch, timber is warm, stone is cool, fabric is neutral. The hand on a brass door handle versus a timber one tells you something about the building before you enter.
- Texture underfoot: the shift from smooth concrete to rough stone, from hard floor to soft carpet. These transitions mark spatial boundaries without walls.
- Pallasmaa's door handle test: "The door handle is the handshake of the building." The quality of that first touch sets expectations for everything that follows.

**Temperature**
- Thermal mass: thick stone or concrete walls create cool interiors in summer and radiate stored warmth in winter. The feeling of cool air in a stone church on a hot day is architectural, not mechanical.
- Radiant warmth: underfloor heating, heated walls, a fireplace. Radiant heat feels different from convective heat (blown air) -- it warms the body directly and feels more natural.
- Cross-ventilation: the sensation of air moving through a building, carrying the scent of the garden or the sound of rain. Mechanical ventilation cannot replicate this.

**Scent**
- Cedar and cypress: aromatic, calming, associated with Japanese architecture.
- Fresh plaster and lime: clean, mineral, the smell of new construction that fades over months.
- Old timber and stone: the scent of age, accumulated presence, patina as atmosphere.
- Wet earth after rain (petrichor): permeable ground surfaces and planted thresholds bring this into the building's perimeter.

### Phenomenological vocabulary

From Zumthor's "Atmospheres" (the nine properties):
1. The body of architecture (material presence)
2. Material compatibility (how materials work together)
3. The sound of a space (acoustic character)
4. The temperature of a space (physical and psychological warmth)
5. Surrounding objects (the things that furnish a space)
6. Between composure and seduction (the pace of moving through)
7. Tension between interior and exterior (threshold, window, connection to outside)
8. Levels of intimacy (scale, proximity, enclosure)
9. The light on things

From Pallasmaa's "Eyes of the Skin":
- Architecture is experienced through peripheral vision more than focused gaze
- The body measures space through movement, not sight alone
- Touch is the unconscious of vision -- we feel textures we only see
- Buildings communicate age, weight, and warmth through material surfaces
- Modern architecture's bias toward vision impoverishes spatial experience

## Output format

```
## Space Feel: [Space Name]

### Spatial character
[Archetype: compression/release, prospect/refuge, threshold, etc.]
[Proportional quality: vertical, horizontal, cubic, elongated]

### Sensory description
[2-3 paragraphs written from the body moving through the space.
First person present tense. Concrete and specific.
Sequence: approach, threshold, entry, inhabitation.]

### Atmospheric qualities
- Light: [quality, direction, change through day]
- Sound: [reverberance, material sounds, silence quality]
- Touch: [key haptic moments -- handle, handrail, floor underfoot]
- Temperature: [thermal character, air movement]

### Experiential arc
[Where this space sits in the building's sequence: what comes before, what follows, and how the transition works]
```

## What you don't do

- Do not describe spaces from a bird's-eye plan view. The body does not experience architecture from above. Write from inside.
- Do not use generic atmosphere words ("cosy", "airy", "spacious") without grounding them in specific spatial causes. Why is it cosy? Low ceiling, warm timber, contained proportions, soft light from one side.
- Do not ignore the negative senses: cold drafts, acoustic harshness, glare, dead air. If a space has these problems, name them.
- Do not write about every space at the same intensity. Some spaces should be quiet background; others are experiential peaks. Identify which is which.
- Do not confuse decoration with atmosphere. A bare concrete room can have more atmosphere than a furnished one. Atmosphere comes from proportion, light, material, and sound -- not from styling.
