# /concept-narrative

Write a design rationale or concept statement for architectural presentations, competition submissions, or planning applications. Translate spatial decisions into compelling written arguments.

## When to use

- Writing a design statement for a competition entry or portfolio
- Preparing text for a planning submission or design review panel
- Framing a project for a client presentation
- Articulating the "why" behind design decisions that the drawings alone don't explain

## Input

- Project description: what the building is, where it sits, who it serves
- Key design moves: the 2-4 decisions that define the project
- Audience: client, competition jury, planning board, publication, or general public
- Material and spatial strategies already decided
- Any site-specific narrative (history, landscape, community context)
- Tone preference (if any)

## Process

1. Identify the strongest narrative thread from the input. Every good project has one core idea -- find it.
2. Choose a narrative structure (see below) that fits the project and audience.
3. Write from the specific to the general. Start with a concrete observation about site, material, or experience. Build toward the bigger idea.
4. Adjust register for the audience: planning boards want demonstrated compliance wrapped in vision; juries want intellectual ambition; clients want to see their brief reflected back with added value.
5. Edit ruthlessly. Remove every adjective that doesn't carry weight. Remove every sentence that restates the previous one.

## Domain knowledge

### Narrative structures

**The site story**: the design emerges from a close reading of place. Topography, orientation, views, neighbouring context, microclimate, or historical layers become the generative logic. Works best for projects on strong sites or in sensitive contexts.
Example framing: "The building reads the slope..." / "The plan follows the line of the former..." / "The section opens toward..."

**The material story**: one material or construction logic drives the entire design. The choice of material determines form, detail, and spatial quality. Works best for projects with a clear tectonic proposition.
Example framing: "The building is made of one thing..." / "Everything follows from the decision to build in..." / "The timber frame is both structure and finish..."

**The light story**: the manipulation of natural light is the primary design strategy. Orientation, aperture, depth, and reflection create the spatial experience. Works best for contemplative, cultural, or religious buildings.
Example framing: "Light enters from above..." / "The section is a device for catching low winter sun..." / "As you move through the building, light shifts from..."

**The experience story**: a choreographed sequence of spatial events. Compression and release, threshold, reveal, prospect and refuge. The narrative follows a body moving through the building. Works best for museums, houses, and any project with a clear procession.
Example framing: "You arrive at..." / "The corridor narrows before..." / "The first thing you see is..."

**The programme story**: the organisation of activities generates the architecture. The social logic of who does what where, and how activities relate, drives the plan. Works best for complex briefs (schools, hospitals, co-housing).
Example framing: "The building separates noisy from quiet..." / "The plan clusters making activities around a shared..." / "Children arrive at one threshold; the public at another..."

### Audience calibration

**Client presentations**: foreground their brief. Show that you listened. Use their language where appropriate, then elevate it. Keep sentences short. Lead with experience, not theory. Avoid jargon. The goal is confidence -- they need to trust you understood what they asked for and added something they didn't expect.

**Competition juries**: demonstrate intellectual rigour and spatial ambition. Reference precedent without name-dropping. Show awareness of the site's cultural and urban context. Be precise about what is new in your approach. Juries read 50-200 submissions -- your first paragraph must be sharp enough to earn the second.

**Planning boards / design review**: frame the design as a response to context. Use planning policy language ("enhancing the character of the conservation area", "responding to the scale and rhythm of the streetscape"). Be specific about materials, heights, and setbacks. Avoid poetic language -- it reads as evasion to planning officers. The goal is approval, not admiration.

**Publications / portfolio**: find the one idea that makes this project worth publishing. Not what it is, but what it argues. Position it within a discourse. Be concise -- editors cut, so write tight.

### Tone guidance

**Confident, not arrogant**: "The building steps down the hillside" not "We have brilliantly resolved the challenging topography." The design should speak, not the designer.

**Specific, not vague**: "The south wall is 600mm rammed earth that stores heat through the afternoon and radiates it into the evening" not "The building responds to its environment." Specificity is credibility.

**Active voice**: "The roof extends over the terrace" not "The terrace is covered by an extension of the roof." Active voice makes the building feel intentional, not accidental.

**No empty adjectives**: delete "stunning", "iconic", "unique", "innovative", "state-of-the-art", "world-class." These words say nothing. If the design is good, the description of what it does will convey that.

**Phenomenological grounding**: when describing experience, draw on the language of Zumthor ("Atmospheres" -- temperature, material compatibility, tension between interior and exterior, levels of intimacy), Pallasmaa ("Eyes of the Skin" -- haptic experience, peripheral vision, acoustic space, the body's memory), and Holl ("Questions of Perception" -- parallax, enmeshed experience, chromatic space). Use their concepts, not their exact phrases.

### Common failure modes in concept writing

- **The manifesto**: grand claims about "redefining" or "reimagining" a typology. Unless the project genuinely does this, it reads as bluster.
- **The apology**: over-explaining constraints. "Despite the limited budget..." weakens the work. Present decisions as choices, not compromises.
- **The inventory**: listing every room and material without connecting them to an idea. A concept statement is not a specification.
- **The abstraction**: all concept, no building. "The project explores the boundary between public and private" means nothing without a description of how.
- **The chronology**: "First we analysed the site, then we developed options, then we refined..." Process is not narrative. Start with the result.

## Output format

```
## Design Statement: [Project Name]

### [Opening line -- concrete, site-specific, sets the tone]

[2-3 paragraphs structured around the chosen narrative thread.
Paragraph 1: the core idea and its origin (site, brief, material, light).
Paragraph 2: how the idea manifests in spatial organisation and material strategy.
Paragraph 3: the experiential result -- what it feels like to be in the building.]

### Key moves
- [Design move 1]: [one-sentence explanation]
- [Design move 2]: [one-sentence explanation]
- [Design move 3]: [one-sentence explanation]
```

Length: 150-400 words for a presentation statement. 500-800 for a competition submission. 100-200 for a planning application summary. Adjust to audience.

## What you don't do

- Do not write in the first person plural ("We believe...", "Our vision..."). Write about the building, not the architect.
- Do not use superlatives. Let the work be assessed on its own terms.
- Do not front-load with context paragraphs about climate crisis, urbanisation, or housing shortage unless the project directly addresses them. Generic preamble wastes the reader's goodwill.
- Do not describe what the drawings already show. The text should add a layer of understanding that the images alone cannot communicate.
- Do not fabricate design intent. If a decision was pragmatic (budget, code, client preference), say so honestly. Pragmatic decisions framed as poetic choices are always detectable and always undermine trust.
