# /precedent-research

Find relevant architectural precedents for a design problem. Match by typology, spatial strategy, material approach, climate response, or programmatic challenge -- not by visual similarity alone.

## When to use

- Starting a project and needing reference points for a typology or spatial strategy
- A client asks "what else has been done like this?" and you need informed answers
- Building a case study section for a competition entry or design report
- Challenging your own design assumptions by studying how others solved the same problem
- Establishing credibility in a planning application by demonstrating awareness of relevant built work

## Input

- Project type (building typology)
- Key design challenges (steep site, deep plan, adaptive reuse, low budget, extreme climate)
- Spatial strategies under consideration (courtyard, split-level, enfilade, megastructure)
- Material or structural approach
- Scale: approximate floor area, number of storeys, number of units
- Geography or climate context
- Time period preference: historical precedents, contemporary, or both

## Process

1. Parse the input to identify the primary matching dimension: is this a typology search, a spatial strategy search, a material search, or a climate/context search?
2. Cross-reference against the precedent database below and broader architectural knowledge.
3. Select 3-6 precedents that illuminate different aspects of the design problem. Avoid selecting 6 buildings that all say the same thing.
4. For each precedent, identify the specific lesson: what did this project solve that is relevant to the current design?
5. Note what does NOT transfer: scale, climate, budget, or cultural context differences that limit direct applicability.
6. Sequence precedents from most directly relevant to most laterally useful.

## Domain knowledge

### Matching dimensions

**Typology match**: same building type. Useful but often superficial -- two houses can have nothing in common beyond being houses. Always pair typology with at least one other dimension.

**Spatial strategy match**: same organisational logic regardless of programme. A courtyard house and a courtyard school share spatial DNA. A split-section museum and a split-section school solve the same sectional problem. This is the most productive matching dimension.

**Material/tectonic match**: same construction logic. A rammed earth house and a rammed earth winery share construction knowledge, thermal behaviour, and aesthetic language. Material precedents are especially useful for unfamiliar construction techniques.

**Climate response match**: same latitude, temperature range, or humidity condition. A building in Lisbon is more relevant to a project in Los Angeles than a building in London, regardless of typology. Climate response precedents reveal passive strategies.

**Scale match**: similar building volume, footprint, or unit count. A 4-unit housing project has more to learn from other 4-unit projects than from a 400-unit masterplan, even if the typology is the same.

**Budget/constraint match**: similar resource limitations. Social housing projects learn more from other social housing than from luxury villas. Constraint-driven design produces different lessons than unconstrained design.

### Reference precedents by typology

**Residential -- houses**
- Villa Savoye, Le Corbusier, Poissy, 1931. The five points of architecture demonstrated. Free plan, pilotis, roof garden, ribbon windows, free facade. The canonical modern house, still instructive on the relationship between structure and spatial freedom.
- Farnsworth House, Mies van der Rohe, Plano IL, 1951. Universal space. Steel and glass reduced to minimum. The tension between transparency and inhabitability. Lesson: simplicity in architecture is not simple to achieve.
- Casa Barragan, Luis Barragan, Mexico City, 1948. Introverted courtyard house. Colour, light, and water as primary materials. Thick walls, filtered views, emotional rather than rational space. Lesson: architecture can be private, sensory, and non-didactic.
- Fallingwater, Frank Lloyd Wright, Mill Run PA, 1935. Building as landscape extension. Cantilevered terraces over a waterfall. Horizontal planes, natural stone, integration of building and site. Lesson: site engagement as design generator.
- Muller House, Adolf Loos, Prague, 1930. Raumplan. Interlocking half-levels create spatial richness within a compact volume. Each room has its own ceiling height and character. Lesson: section as design tool.
- Villa Dall'Ava, OMA/Rem Koolhaas, Paris, 1991. Subversive modernism. Pilotis holding up a heavy box. Swimming pool on the roof with Eiffel Tower view. Playful, contradictory, uncomfortable. Lesson: rules are for breaking (if you understand them first).
- House NA, Sou Fujimoto, Tokyo, 2011. 21 floor planes at different levels within a transparent frame. Living as climbing. Privacy through spatial complexity rather than enclosure. Lesson: radical rethinking of domestic convention.

**Residential -- multi-unit**
- Unite d'Habitation, Le Corbusier, Marseille, 1952. Vertical city. Internal streets, duplex units, communal roof terrace. Beton brut. Lesson: collective housing as urban proposition.
- Gifu Kitagata, SANAA, Japan, 2000. Minimal apartment building. Open terraces as extension of living space. Transparency and lightness. Lesson: less can genuinely be more in social housing.
- 8 House, BIG, Copenhagen, 2010. Figure-8 loop with a continuous path from street to roof. Mixed programme: apartments, offices, retail. Lesson: diagram as architecture (for better or worse).
- Robin Hood Gardens, Alison and Peter Smithson, London, 1972. Streets in the sky. Brutalist social housing as urban experiment. Now demolished. Lesson: spatial generosity in social housing, and the gap between architectural intention and social reality.

**Cultural -- museums and galleries**
- Guggenheim Bilbao, Frank Gehry, 1997. Titanium-clad sculptural form. The "Bilbao effect" -- architecture as economic catalyst. Large-scale, flowing galleries. Lesson: architecture's power to transform a city's identity (and the danger of trying to replicate it).
- Jewish Museum Berlin, Daniel Libeskind, 2001. Architecture as narrative. Zigzag plan, voids, disorientation. The building tells a story through spatial experience before any exhibit is installed. Lesson: form can carry meaning independent of programme.
- Tate Modern, Herzog & de Meuron, London, 2000. Industrial conversion. The Turbine Hall as found space. Minimal intervention with maximum impact. Lesson: existing buildings have qualities new construction cannot replicate.
- Kimbell Art Museum, Louis Kahn, Fort Worth, 1972. Cycloid concrete vaults with continuous skylights. Natural light as the primary exhibition medium. Lesson: structure, space, and light as a unified system.
- Kunsthaus Bregenz, Peter Zumthor, 1997. Glass, steel, and concrete. Translucent glass skin admits diffuse light. Concrete floors are both structure and exhibition surface. Lesson: material reduction creates spatial intensity.
- Neues Museum, David Chipperfield, Berlin, 2009. Reconstruction of a war-damaged museum. New construction reads as clearly new alongside preserved ruins. Lesson: how to work with history without pastiche.

**Religious and contemplative**
- Notre Dame du Haut (Ronchamp), Le Corbusier, 1955. Sculptural concrete chapel. Thick walls perforated with irregular windows. Light as spiritual medium. Lesson: architecture's capacity for the numinous.
- Bruder Klaus Field Chapel, Peter Zumthor, 2007. Rammed concrete exterior, charred timber interior void. Oculus open to rain and sky. Made by local farmers. Lesson: material process as architecture.
- Church of the Light, Tadao Ando, Osaka, 1989. A concrete box with a cruciform slot. Nothing else. Lesson: restraint as power.
- Woodland Cemetery Chapel, Gunnar Asplund, Stockholm, 1920. Small chapel in a forest clearing. Classical portico, dark interior, landscape as sacred setting. Lesson: threshold between worlds.

**Education**
- Hunstanton School, Alison and Peter Smithson, 1954. "New Brutalism" origin. Exposed steel, brick, and services. Honest construction as pedagogical statement. Lesson: legible construction.
- Rolex Learning Center, SANAA, Lausanne, 2010. Single undulating concrete slab. No walls, no corridors -- landscape as plan. Lesson: radical spatial continuity.
- Escola da Talalua, Diebedo Francis Kere, Burkina Faso, 2001. Compressed earth blocks, ventilated double roof, built with community labour. Lesson: constraint-driven innovation and the social contract of building.

**Civic and infrastructure**
- Salk Institute, Louis Kahn, La Jolla, 1965. Research labs flanking a travertine plaza open to the Pacific. Study towers in concrete and teak. Lesson: institutional building as place of contemplation.
- Pompidou Centre, Piano + Rogers, Paris, 1977. Inside-out building. Structure and services on the exterior. Flexible, column-free interiors. Lesson: radical legibility and the building as machine.
- Therme Vals, Peter Zumthor, 1996. Stone bath complex built into a hillside. Local Valser quartzite. Light enters through gaps between stone slabs. Lesson: architecture as the choreography of bathing, light, and stone.

### How to use precedents well

- **Extract the principle, not the form.** Copying a Zumthor detail without understanding why he made it produces kitsch. Understand the spatial logic, then find your own formal expression.
- **Acknowledge the differences.** A precedent in Osaka does not transfer directly to Oslo. Name the climate, cultural, budgetary, and regulatory differences.
- **Use precedents to challenge, not just confirm.** If all your precedents agree with your design, you're in a confirmation bubble. Include at least one that contradicts your approach and explain why you're diverging.
- **Date matters.** A building from 1955 was designed under different thermal regulations, accessibility standards, and cost structures. Precedent teaches spatial and tectonic thinking, not specification.

## Output format

```
## Precedent Research: [Project Name / Design Question]

### Search dimensions
- Primary match: [typology / spatial strategy / material / climate / scale]
- Secondary match: [second dimension]

### Precedents

#### 1. [Building Name], [Architect], [Location], [Year]
- **Relevance**: [why this project matters for the current design]
- **Key lesson**: [the specific transferable insight]
- **Limitations**: [what does NOT transfer -- scale, climate, budget, context]
- **Source**: [monograph, El Croquis issue, Archdaily, Divisare]

#### 2. [Building Name], [Architect], [Location], [Year]
...

[Repeat for 3-6 precedents]

### Synthesis
[2-3 sentences on what the precedent set collectively suggests for the design direction]
```

## What you don't do

- Do not select precedents based solely on visual resemblance. A building that looks similar but solves a different problem teaches nothing.
- Do not list only canonical buildings. Mix landmark precedents with lesser-known projects that may be more directly relevant in scale, budget, or context.
- Do not present precedents without critique. Every building has compromises and failures. Name them.
- Do not use precedents as authority arguments ("Kahn did it, so it must be right"). Use them as evidence that a spatial strategy works in practice, then evaluate whether the conditions match.
- Do not ignore non-Western precedents. The bias toward European and North American examples in architectural education is a limitation, not a standard. Include precedents from the relevant geographic and climatic context.
