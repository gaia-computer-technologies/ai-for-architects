# /design-critique

Provide structured, constructive design feedback on an architectural project. Evaluate against the project's own stated intentions, not abstract ideals.

## When to use

- Self-review before submitting a design for crit, client presentation, or competition
- Peer review of a colleague's work when they ask for feedback
- Identifying blind spots in a design that "feels off" but the reason is unclear
- Preparing for a formal design review or jury by anticipating questions
- Teaching contexts: helping students develop critical thinking about their own work

## Input

- Design material: plans, sections, elevations, renders, model photos, or written descriptions
- Project brief or stated design intent (crucial -- critique is relative to intent)
- Stage of design: concept, developed design, or technical
- What kind of feedback is requested: overall coherence, specific concerns, presentation quality, or all of the above
- Context: is this a student project, competition entry, real commission, or speculative work?

## Process

1. Read the stated intent first. The question is not "is this good architecture?" but "does this design achieve what it set out to do?"
2. Evaluate across the five critique dimensions below, noting strengths before weaknesses.
3. For each weakness, identify the root cause (not just the symptom) and suggest a direction for resolution.
4. Calibrate the level of critique to the design stage. At concept stage, don't critique window details. At technical stage, don't suggest plan reorganisation.
5. Frame feedback as questions where possible. "What happens to this corridor in section?" is more productive than "this corridor is too narrow."
6. Distinguish between personal preference and genuine design issues. Flag which is which.

## Domain knowledge

### The five critique dimensions

**1. Spatial coherence**
Does the plan have a clear organisational logic? Can you read the hierarchy of spaces? Is there a legible sequence from entry to the principal spaces?

What to look for:
- Entry sequence: is arrival clear? Does the building tell you where to go without signage?
- Circulation logic: is movement through the building purposeful or residual? Are corridors spaces in their own right or leftover gaps between rooms?
- Section relationships: do the levels relate to each other? Is there vertical spatial connection where it matters (double-height living, gallery voids, visual links)?
- Plan-section agreement: does the plan promise something the section doesn't deliver? A grand plan with a flat section is a missed opportunity.
- Spatial hierarchy: which is the most important space? Is that clear from its size, position, light, and height? If all spaces are equal, none are special.

Common problems:
- The "corridor problem": a plan that is all corridor and small rooms, with no spatial generosity anywhere. Usually caused by programme-driven planning without sectional thinking.
- The "front-back split": the entrance facade is composed, the rear is a mess. This reveals that the design is graphic (elevation-driven) rather than spatial.
- Missing threshold: jumping from outside to inside with no transition. A porch, a lobby, a change in ceiling height -- something needs to mediate.

**2. Material logic**
Does the material palette make sense as a system? Are material choices driven by spatial intent, or applied as decoration?

What to look for:
- Consistency: does the same material appear in the same conditions throughout? If timber is used for warmth, is it in all the warm spaces or randomly placed?
- Tectonic honesty: do the materials express how the building is constructed? Or is the structure hidden behind a false skin? Both are valid positions, but the choice should be conscious.
- Joint logic: how do materials meet each other? Shadow gaps, expressed fixings, concealed joints -- each communicates a different level of craft and a different attitude to construction.
- Budget alignment: do the specified materials match the project's budget reality? Specifying marble on a plasterboard budget is not aspirational, it's delusional.

Common problems:
- "Material tourism": sampling one of everything (a bit of timber here, some stone there, a metal accent) without a governing logic.
- Render-driven material choices: selecting materials because they look good in a render, not because they work in construction. Smooth white surfaces in renders often become streaky, stained surfaces in reality.
- Ignoring the back: beautiful front facade, fibre cement board rear. If you can't afford good materials everywhere, use one honest material consistently.

**3. Light strategy**
Is natural light used intentionally? Does the section work with the orientation to deliver good light to the right spaces?

What to look for:
- Orientation awareness: are the main living/working spaces oriented for appropriate light? Is the plan adapted to the site's solar conditions?
- Section as light device: does the section create opportunities for borrowed light, clerestories, or rooflights? A flat ceiling throughout is a missed opportunity.
- Glare and overheating: are west-facing full-height windows unshaded? Is there a thermal strategy for large glazed areas?
- Dark spaces: are servant spaces (storage, WCs, plant) placed where they don't need light, freeing the perimeter for occupied rooms?

Common problems:
- "Glass box syndrome": full glazing on all sides regardless of orientation. Looks transparent in renders, causes glare, overheating, and privacy loss in reality.
- Dark centre: deep plans with no strategy for getting light to the core. If the plan is more than 12-15m deep, it needs rooflights, courtyards, or light wells.
- Ignoring artificial light: the natural light strategy ends at sunset. What happens then? If the answer is "downlights everywhere," the spatial experience collapses.

**4. Programme fit**
Does the spatial organisation serve the activities it houses? Are the sizes, adjacencies, and relationships appropriate?

What to look for:
- Brief compliance: does every required space exist, at an appropriate size? Count the rooms against the brief.
- Adjacency logic: are related spaces close? Are conflicting uses separated? Is the wet-room plumbing stacked?
- Storage: is there enough? There is almost never enough. Check.
- Flexibility: can the spaces adapt to changing use over time? A room that can only be one thing is brittle.
- Furniture test: can the spaces actually be furnished? Draw beds, desks, sofas at scale. A 3m-wide bedroom with a door in the middle of one wall cannot fit a double bed and wardrobes.

Common problems:
- "The beautiful plan that doesn't work": spatially elegant but programmatically illiterate. Bathrooms far from bedrooms, kitchens with no natural light, living rooms you walk through to reach every other room.
- Over-programming: every square metre assigned to a specific function, leaving no slack. Buildings need some ambiguous space -- a wide landing, a generous hallway, a room without a name.
- Ignoring servicing: where does the boiler go? The bins? The electricity meter? The incoming services? These prosaic requirements destroy elegant plans when addressed late.

**5. Presentation clarity**
Is the design communicated effectively? Can someone unfamiliar with the project understand it from the presentation material?

What to look for:
- Drawing hierarchy: is the most important drawing the largest and most prominent? Or is everything the same size, creating visual democracy but narrative confusion?
- Consistent graphic language: do plans, sections, and elevations use the same line weights, hatching, and notation? Inconsistency suggests the drawings were produced hastily.
- Scale figures: are there people in the drawings? Without them, scale is illegible. People also communicate programme and atmosphere.
- Key views: do the perspectives/renders show the most important spatial experiences? Or do they show the easiest view to draw?
- Text-drawing relationship: does the written narrative describe what the drawings show? Or do text and drawings tell different stories?

Common problems:
- "The beauty contest": presentation quality masks design weakness. Beautiful renders of a mediocre plan. Sophisticated graphic style hiding unresolved junctions.
- Missing section: the project is presented entirely in plan and 3D, with no section. This usually means the section hasn't been designed. The section is where architecture lives.
- Over-rendered: so many renders that you can't find the plan. Renders show what a building looks like; plans and sections show how it works. Both are needed.

### Giving constructive criticism

**Be specific**: "the entrance is unclear" is useless. "The entrance is recessed behind a column that blocks the view from the street, and the door is the same material as the adjacent wall" is actionable.

**Ground it in the project's goals**: "you said this project is about connection to landscape, but the living room faces the car park" is powerful because it uses the designer's own stated intent as the benchmark.

**Offer a direction, not a solution**: "consider what happens if you rotate the plan 90 degrees" is better than "rotate the plan 90 degrees." The designer needs to solve it, not execute your solution.

**Distinguish severity**: some issues are fundamental (the plan doesn't work), some are significant (the material palette is incoherent), some are minor (the window proportions could be refined). Don't present all feedback at the same urgency.

**Name what works**: start with genuine strengths. This is not politeness -- it identifies what the design should protect as it evolves. "The section through the living space is excellent -- make sure plan revisions don't compromise that double-height volume."

### Common blind spots by experience level

**Student / early career**:
- Plan fixation: designing in plan only, discovering the section too late. The section is where architecture happens.
- Reference dependency: the design looks like the precedent rather than learning from it. Extract the principle, not the form.
- Scale blindness: a 1m-wide corridor or a 2m-diameter spiral stair drawn at 1:500 looks fine. At 1:50, the problem is obvious. Zoom in.
- Over-complexity: trying to do everything in one building. The strongest designs usually commit to one idea.
- Detail avoidance: stopping at the diagram level. How does the wall meet the ground? How does the window open? These questions reveal whether the design is buildable.

**Mid-career**:
- Comfort zone: repeating a successful formula. The third courtyard house might be refined, but is it still investigating anything?
- Client capture: letting the client's taste override spatial quality. The architect's job is to deliver what the client needs, which is not always what they ask for.
- Trend sensitivity: adopting formal or material trends without interrogating whether they serve the specific project.

```
## Design Critique: [Project Name]

### Overall assessment
[2-3 sentences: the project's primary strength and primary concern]

### Strengths
- [Specific strength with evidence from the design]
- [Another strength]

### Critique by dimension

#### Spatial coherence: [strong / developing / needs work]
[Specific observations and questions]

#### Material logic: [strong / developing / needs work]
[Specific observations and questions]

#### Light strategy: [strong / developing / needs work]
[Specific observations and questions]

#### Programme fit: [strong / developing / needs work]
[Specific observations and questions]

#### Presentation clarity: [strong / developing / needs work]
[Specific observations and questions]

### Priority actions
1. [Most important thing to address -- fundamental]
2. [Second priority -- significant]
3. [Third priority -- refinement]

### Questions for the designer
- [Open question that might unlock a resolution]
- [Another question]
```

## What you don't do

- Do not critique style. "I would have designed it differently" is not feedback. Evaluate the design against its own logic and intentions.
- Do not suggest starting over. Even deeply flawed designs contain something worth saving. Find it and build from there.
- Do not give feedback on everything simultaneously. Prioritise. Three actionable points are better than fifteen observations.
- Do not assume the designer hasn't considered something just because it isn't visible in the presentation. Ask before asserting.
- Do not be cruel. Architectural education has a toxic tradition of brutal crits that humiliate rather than teach. Rigorous critique is not the same as harsh critique. Be direct, be specific, be respectful.
